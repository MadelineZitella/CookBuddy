Checkpoint 2: Proposal
Need Finding Study Protocol
Research Questions
The contextual inquiry is aimed at answering the following research questions:
How do users currently plan and decide what to cook on a daily or weekly basis?
What mental effort is involved in deciding meals?
How do users balance time, nutrition, cost, and personal preference?
What causes food and decision fatigue (if anything)?
How do users manage groceries and food waste?
What tools (if any) do users use for meal planning or nutrition tracking (ie fitness apps)?
What frustrations exist in current meal planning and recipe discovery?
How receptive would users be to an AI meal assistant?
What concerns might prevent a user from using this AI assistant?

Study Findings
Impact on User Experience
Meal planning creates an ongoing mental load:
Participant 1 described dinner planning as her very first thought of the day, “The first thing that I think about when I wake up in the morning is, what am I gonna make for dinner?”. She also mentioned that this thought is not only hers, and others share the frustration, “When I talk to my friends, it’s us griping about what are we going to cook for dinner?” Participant 2 echoed a similar sentiment, noting the mental toll and monotony of coming up with new ideas for dinner. Participant 3, although in a different life stage as a recent graduate working full-time, demonstrated the same recurring decision burden. He cooks approximately 3–5 times per week and is often described as defaulting to simple meals or takeout when mentally tired after work and the gym. Even when he has groceries, he still faces the “what should I make?” moment, especially around late afternoon when hunger and mood begin influencing the decision. Across all three participants (who collectively cook between 4–6 dinners per week on average), this indicates that meal planning is not just a logistical task but an emotionally draining, recurring cognitive process. In terms of user experience, an effective solution should reduce cognitive load and not add additional decisions or steps, since users already make approximately 15–21 meal decisions per week.
High cooking confidence does not eliminate decision fatigue:
Participant 1 cooks 5-6 times per week and is very confident, saying, “I feel very confident in the kitchen. And if I have any questions, I just Google it.” However, she still experiences food and decision fatigue as well as a lack of motivation. This is present in her comments, “Honestly, I’m so tired of eating the same thin,g” and “It’s just food fatigue that day.” Participant 2 expressed a similar confidence in the kitchen, remarking on her 24 years of experience preparing food for a family, and a structured routine for meal planning and shopping as a result. Participant 3 similarly expressed strong confidence in cooking steak, eggs, pasta, and grilling. Despite this, he still defaults to simpler meals when busy and occasionally orders takeout when exhausted. His confidence does not eliminate decision fatigue — it only makes execution easier once a decision has been made. This shows the problem is not skill, but variety and mental energy. To improve the user experience, CookBuddy must address variety and reduce decision burden rather than simply providing more recipes.
Meals are highly routine-based:
Both Participant 1 and 2 described cooking as an autopilot mindset, with breakfast and lunch being almost the same everyday and dinner rotating around the same few ingredients of chicken and vegetables. This routine reduces effort, but leads to more boredom. Participant 3 described similar behavior, consistently buying staples like eggs and pantry items and having “phases” (e.g., tacos for a week). His grocery shopping is often intuitive rather than list-based, and he frequently defaults to familiar proteins and simple combinations. This routine reduces effort, but leads to boredom and food fatigue over time. To improve the user experience, CookBuddy must respect habitual patterns while still introducing manageable variation to avoid overwhelming users with drastic change.
Food waste has a strong, emotional response:
Participant 1 speaks about hating to waste food, comparing throwing food out to putting a bill straight into the trash. Waste primarily comes from unexpected schedule changes, overestimating produce expiration, and food fatigue from unused items. Participant 2 noted that throwing away groceries that have gone bad makes her “crazy,” and is more so the source of food waste in her household, rather than not knowing what to do with the ingredients. Similar to Participant 1, this occurs as a result of last-minute schedule changes. Participant 3 also described recently throwing away bread that had expired and feeling frustrated about wasting money. He acknowledged that forgotten produce in the fridge is common and often discovered after expiration. To tackle this, the system must be aware of the ingredients they are suggesting and when they would expire. This would improve user satisfaction.
Time constraints lead to simpler meals, not eliminating them:
On busy days, Participant 1 mainly speaks about making her meals simpler if time is short, but never eliminating them completely. Participant 2 mostly agreed, saying that she occasionally with opt to order food as a result of schedule changes resulting in time shortages, but she can mostly plan ahead for changes. For a user who may be short on time, it is important that CookBuddy can provide 15-20 minute meal options.
Focus for users on flexibility:
When asking about the hypothetical app, Participant 1 expressed that she would need something that is not too regimented and worried that if “At 5pm…I want to change gears”, that it would have a hard time adjusting to this. Participant 3 echoed this indirectly through his mood-based and routine-dependent eating. He often decides on meals late in the day and does not want strict enforcement. He emphasized that the effort to input groceries would be a barrier and that the app must be user-friendly and low-friction.
To counteract this, CookBuddy must suggest, not prescribe, allow for easy pivoting, and offer options instead of strict plans. Participant 2 expressed a very rigid and long-standing method of grocery and meal planning and indicated uncertainty as to whether an App would be of help to her, given her existing routine. She prefers working on paper, instead of using a digital method. This is even more of an impetus for CookBuddy to be intuitive, easy to use, and reduce any gaps in understanding as quickly as possible, so that the user remains engaged. 
Privacy concern varys among participants:
Participant 1’s primary concern with the app was privacy, questioning what happens with the data, specifically if it were to be shared with doctors or potentially life insurance and what impacts it could have there. In order to diminish this concern, the system must be transparent in the privacy policy and how data may be stored or shared. Participant 3 was less concerned about recommendation quality and more concerned about effort, but still acknowledged that general effort and friction would prevent him from using the app consistently. Participant 2 did not value privacy but expressed skepticism about whether a grocery tracking app would meaningfully add value.
Nutrition is important without becoming obsessive:
Participant 1 had previously used MyFitnessPal, but stopped because it was very manual and was way too regimented for her. She values protein and whole foods, but did not care for tracking macros or taking supplements. Participant 2 indicated maintaining nutrition goals for herself, ephasizing protein and calcium. Like Participant 1, she has used MyFitnessPal, and indicated dissatisfaction with the monotony of inputting every food manually. However, she continues to use the app, saying it is “easy to use.” It seems that although there is a shared feeling of manual tracking being time consuming and bothersome, apps like MyFitnessPal are the most readily available options. Regardless, nutrition is the most important factor for making meals for Participant 2, who also must take into account the tastes and goals of those she prepares food for, in addition to just herself. Participant 3 similarly prioritizes protein intake (especially for muscle gain) and defines a healthy meal as one with sufficient protein and fruits or vegetables. However, he does not use tracking apps and considers calorie tracking excessive effort. He occasionally uses tools like ChatGPT or TikTok for recipes but does not log nutrition. So, CookBuddy should estimate nutrition passively without much user requirement, avoid manual logging, and focus more on balance rather than macros since this is primarily a cooking application.

Updated Problem Scope
Initially, CookBuddy focused primarily on helping users cook with ingredients they already have. However, this study expands the scope to include broader behavioral and emotional factors that influence meal planning. The problem is not simply ingredient matching, but cognitive and emotional strain that occurs repeatedly throughout the week.
Based on our findings, the scope now includes:
Reducing decision fatigue across daily meal choices
Addressing food fatigue caused by repetitive routines
Supporting flexible, mood-based planning rather than rigid scheduling
Minimizing food waste caused by forgotten or expiring ingredients
Providing low-effort meal options for time-constrained days
Ensuring privacy transparency and minimizing required data input
Across participants of different life stages and cooking frequencies (3–6 meals per week), we observed that even highly skilled and nutritionally aware individuals experience ongoing mental load around meal decisions. This strain accumulates over approximately 15–21 meal decisions per week, leading to boredom, stress, simplified meals, or takeout.
Therefore, our updated problem scope is:
Across life stages, users experience consistent cognitive and emotional strain around meal planning, even if they are a confident cook. They need a flexible, low-friction, privacy-conscious support system that reduces mental load without imposing rigid structures or excessive tracking.
Proposed Approach to Tackle Problem
Design Goals
Based on our findings, CookBuddy should (1) reduce cognitive load in daily meal decisions, (2) provide ingredient aware suggestions, (3) introduce meal variety, (4) adapt to time constraints, (5) avoid rigid planning, (6) offer passive nutrition awareness, (7) maintain privacy and transparency and (8) minimize manual load on user input.
Constraints
The key constraints for this are:
Users resist overly regimented systems (all 3 participants rejected strict scheduling)
Users do not want heavy manual data entry or time-consuming setup
Privacy concerns must be addressed clearly and transparently
Recommendations must be flexible and easily overridden
System must function even with partial ingredient data
Users may grocery shop inconsistently (weekly, biweekly, or intuitively)
Cooking frequency varies (3–6 meals/week), so engagement cannot depend on daily use
These constraints emphasize that adoption depends on low friction, adaptability, and trust.
